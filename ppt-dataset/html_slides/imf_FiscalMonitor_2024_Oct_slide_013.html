Moreover, the cost of inaction is costly: in countries where debt is projected to increase further—such as China, France, Italy, the United Kingdom, and the United States—lacking adjustment could make fiscal adjustments larger. Waiting is risky; country experiences show that high debt can trigger adverse market reactions and constrain room for budgetary maneuver in the face of negative shocks. Key features of the needed fiscal adjustments involve the following:

  • Identifying the size: Countries need adjustments of 3.0–4.5 percent of GDP, on average, is needed to stabilize or reduce debt with high probability. The magnitude of the required fiscal adjustment is higher than that currently planned, and almost twice the size of past adjustments, especially in those countries where debt is not projected to stabilize. In countries with more benign debt outlooks, optimizing fiscal space while maintaining debt sustainability is a priority.
  • Designing the composition: Careful design of fiscal adjustment can prevent countries from falling into a prolonged period of anemic growth. Key elements of fiscal adjustment vary across countries. Advanced economies should reprioritize expenditures, reform entitlement reforms, increase revenues through indirect taxes where capacity is low, and reduce inefficient tax incentives. Emerging market and developing economies, where there is room, should raise revenues while limiting the burden of broadening tax bases, including by reducing exemptions and improving revenue administration capacity. On the expenditure side, efforts to rationalize spending include subsidy cuts, strengthen social safety nets, and safeguard public investment because public investment crowds out output while protecting vulnerable households, and supporting deficit reductions.
  • Calibrating the pace: Gradual but sustained fiscal adjustment would strike a balance between delivering sufficient fiscal tightening and supporting the strength of private demand. Fast-track consolidation would also deliver large nonlinear gains to tax revenues as well as spending cuts. That said, economies with high risk of debt distress or those that already have lost market access need front-loaded adjustment, although this is a design consideration.
  • Building credibility: Governments need deliberate reforms to strengthen fiscal institutions—medium-term fiscal frameworks and modern public financial management—that can anchor market expectations to fiscal paths and reduce fiscal policy uncertainty. Strong fiscal institutions and oversight can reinforce government credibility.
  • Improving fiscal governance: Countries must avoid underfulfilled debt. Assessing contingent liabilities, including those associated with state-owned enterprises, and monitoring them closely are critical in this regard. On the expenditure side, treasury and active cash management can limit overspending. Governments should also commit to collecting more transparent, granular, and timely information on debt, including the composition of deficit-financed instruments, and exposure to risks.
  • Addressing debt distress: Countries facing debt distress or unsustainable debt, timely and adequate restructuring decisions need to be taken promptly to ensure debt sustainability. Recent DSF reforms aim to hold borrowers more accountable for their commitments to market and bilateral creditors. The Global Sovereign Debt Roundtable, have helped consolidate country commitments and international restructuring timelines. Further strengthening these practices is key to improving efficiency of debt restructuring. To support low-income developing countries, it is also necessary that additional resources are necessary to ensure the provision of concessional financing to avoid undue fiscal tightening.
