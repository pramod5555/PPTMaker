Executive Summary
Soft Landing or Abrupt Awakening?

With core inflation still high and declining only slowly in many advanced economies, central banks may need to keep monetary policy tighter for longer than is currently priced in markets. In emerging market economies, progress on lowering inflation appears to be more advanced, with the benefits of early rate hikes becoming apparent. However, there are discrepancies across regions. Widening divergence of inflation and economic outlook could mark the beginning of the desynchronization of global monetary policy.

Yet, optimism about a soft landing of the global economy, thereby distributing enormous space and a catastrophe avoided, has fueled asset valuations since the April 2023 Global Financial Stability Report. Despite the decline in interest rates since September, driven by rising long-term real rates, financial conditions for financial economies have eased so far (Figure ES.1). Taking a slightly longer view, so far this year, stock prices in Europe and the United States have climbed about 10 and 12 percent, respectively, and corporate credit spreads remain near the lowest levels observed since last year. This benign sentiment has encouraged risk-taking in the aftermath of advanced economies, supported in part by continued monetary policy expectations of an imminent return to balance sheet recession, affecting such as Chile, Hungary, India, Mexico, and Poland have also seen significant local currency appreciation, consistent with the appreciation of most major emerging market currencies in the second half of the year. Upside surprises to the inflation outlook would challenge this soft-landing narrative, resulting in a potentially sharp repricing of assets.

While acute stress in the global banking system has subsided, a weak tail of banks remains in some countries. In additional cracks in other sectors may also become apparent and could turn into more time lags soon. In the event of an abrupt tightening of financial conditions, adverse feedback loops could be triggered and again test the resilience of the global financial system. Most notably, the global credit cycle has started to turn as borrowing debt repayment capacity and global credit growth slows. The IMF's growth-at-risk measure summarizes this assessment: downward risks to global growth have skewed to the downside, similar to the assessment in April 2023 (Figure ES.2). In countries with longer prior financial conditions, such as insurers and pension funds, elevated interest rates have increased the present value of their liabilities and improved funded ratios.
