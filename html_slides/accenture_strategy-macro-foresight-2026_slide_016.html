What to watch for
Asia Pacific
Japan
  • Modest improvement in growth over 2025, with wage gains and consumption offsetting weak external demand
  • Japan is navigating a shifting regional order—benefiting from US-linked supply chains but facing rising competitive pressure from China
  • Wage–price dynamics, especially whether annual Shunto settlements sustain real income growth
  • Market reaction to BOJ tightening steps and yen volatility
  • Exposure of exports and tourism to China/global slowdown
India
  • Slight growth deceleration as tighter household credit and uneven job temper domestic demand, though investment and supply-chain diversification should keep India a relative global outperformer
  • External headwinds—notably US-India trade frictions—pose risk
  • Manufacturing scale-up as firms reassess China exposure
  • Ability to absorb investment (permits, logistics, labor skills) at the pace that reform plans envision
  • Services exports impact from US tech and visa policy shifts
Australia
  • Gradual growth pickup as firmer real incomes and easing inflation offset the drag on investment from still-high borrowing costs
  • Outlook remains closely tied to China's industrial activity, as softer Chinese demand for iron ore and LNG could hamper export momentum
  • Pace of RBA easing and relief for indebted households
  • China’s demand for iron ore and LNG, especially signs of deeper industrial weakness or pivot towards cleaner energy
  • Drag from labor market softening and housing adjustment
Vietnam
  • Continuation of solid growth above ASEAN average, powered by export-oriented manufacturing, FDI and strong tourism
  • Policy focus stays on improving infrastructure, power reliability and capital-market reform to absorb supply-chain diversification flows
  • Impact of US/EU tariffs and China competition on electronics, textiles and other key exports.
  • Progress on infrastructure, energy and SOE reforms that help Vietnam capture near-shoring and FDI momentum
Singapore
  • Growth slows mildly from the 2024–25 rebound amidst weaker external demand, electronics downcycle and tighter global financial conditions
  • Public infrastructure spending and resilient high-value services (logistics, finance, professional services) cushion but not fully offset external drag
  • Sensitivity to global trade tensions and US/China tariffs, especially for electronics and re-exports
  • Progress on workforce upskilling and digitalization to offset ageing and structural cost pressures
Malaysia
  • Moderate growth continues, supported by global semiconductor demand and AI-related investment
  • Consumer spending, however, remains dampened by subsidy reforms, cost of living pressures, and fiscal tightening
  • Implementation of fuel-subsidy reforms and new taxes and their impacts on consumer spending and inflation
  • Sensitivity to domestic demand weakness in China, which is a key export market for Malaysia
Indonesia
  • Continued growth of ~5% driven by domestic demand, infrastructure spending and downstream commodity industries
  • Moderated inflation enables a cautious easing of monetary policy to support growth
  • Continuity of policy support for downstreaming (nickel, EV value chain) and investor sentiment around regulatory shifts
  • Budgetary strain recent social welfare expansion and large infrastructure projects
