Q3:
How do we improve the odds of successful and sustainable transformation?

Successfully scaling requires clear value creation generating real impact for the company intends to go. Three levels of transformation both apply: deploy, reshape, and invent.

Deploy puts AI into existing work. In practice, AI deployment involves rolling out enterprise productivity solutions at scale. Most companies are doing this, and it will quickly become table stakes. The value comes through pervasive productivity gains spread across the organization, which makes impact difficult to isolate and measure as a standalone value case.

Reshape changes how the work is done. The larger, more sustainable productivity gain comes from a small number of priority functions with AI embedded in the process. That means redefining workflows, guardrails, and ways of working.

Invent creates new propositions. Some leading companies use AI to find new business models and change how consumers or customers engage with their business. For CPG companies, that could mean new AI-enabled innovation models that provide personalized advice, routines, or product discovery. For retailers, it could mean an AI-first shopping agent that helps customers plan missions, compare choices, and manage fulfillment across channels.

Q4:
What are the broader impacts on the workforce and the operating model?

As AI shifts the operating model towards reshaping the functions that power the demand-value chain, Work changes significantly across functions — marketing, sales, merchandising, supply chain, data, and technology around shared outcomes. Roles shift toward orchestration, oversight, relationship management, and trade-off decisions. Alongside these shifts, functions look towards engineering capabilities to translate AI into repeatable workflows.

The shift in operating model primarily requires real-time consumer sentiment analytics into concept design and building capabilities, so that marketing becomes part of an innovation process, reducing handoff friction that slows commercial development.

In retail, the merchandising role is shifting. As AI takes on more of the analytical and operational work, merchants will focus increasingly on category strategy, supplier relationships, and commercial judgment calls, and less time stitching together analyses and routing decisions across multiple fragmented processes (See Exhibit 6.).

Companies should be consistent about ecosystem choices when moving towards autopilot ambition. Consequently, leaders need clear ROI data on reshape activities and a decisive view of where and how GenAI solutions will be used. For other functional related areas, such as R&D, quality, and operations, AI and GenAI consumption costs are usually less prominent unless broad deployment or significant cost reduction is at stake. At the investment level, customer-facing propositions such as chatbots and AI agents are more sensitive to cost. As a company's AI use grows, linking cost to key outcomes such as conversion, cost to serve, or retention, becomes more important to choose workflows.
