Investors continue to differentiate between emerging market economies with stronger fundamentals and policy buffers and those considered less resilient and more vulnerable to shocks. Most emerging market central banks have taken no policy tightening actions and have been maintaining their inflation nation despite the continued tightening of monetary policy requiring higher rates. Widening divergence of inflation and investment-grade and high-yield segments of emerging market sovereign debt is also becoming stark. Repayment capacity on credit grades since the pandemic have pulled back on risk, as credit market conditions have widened across many emerging market economies.

As the primary lenders in the global economy, banks are expected to deal with greater credit costs as higher interest rates reduce borrowing capacity of private business. The global banking system appears to have prudently added provisions for loan defaults, and loan loss reserves seem adequate to cover nonperforming loans in many countries. Higher rates should also support net interest income. The analysis in Chapter 2 shows that history has shown that credit provisions have kept the banking system capital ratios generally steady, as observed since the COVID-19 recession, affecting such as Chile, Hungary, India, Mexico, and Poland have also seen. More countries have reported tighter lending standards, private equity funding activity has slowed (as shown in this GFSR ES.4), and the issuance of commercial mortgage-backed securities has gone tepid.
