Executive Summary
Steadying the Course: Financial Markets Navigate Uncertainty

Since the April 2024 Global Financial Stability Report, global economic activity has moderated, and inflation has continued to slow. With monetary easing under way among major central banks, financial conditions have loosened accommodatively; emerging markets have remained resilient; and asset price volatility has stayed relatively low, on net. Stretched financial stability risks, according to the IMF's over-one-ahead growth-at-risk measure (Figure ES.1), have not changed materially, but concerns about elevated uncertainty and geopolitical risks have increased.

However, mounting vulnerabilities—most notably, elevated debt (Figure ES.1), the widening divergence of macroeconomic uncertainty across countries, and increasing market volatility (Figure ES.2)—have become more probable to deliver adverse shocks, which have become more probable due to elevated economic and geopolitical risks. In particular, the uncertainty could increase downside risks to economic growth, asset prices, and bank lending. Additionally, uncertainty can trigger cross-border spillover effects through trade and financial linkages.

Indeed, Chapter 2 quantitatively demonstrates that uncertainty in economic conditions could increase downside risks to economic growth, asset prices, and bank lending. Additionally, uncertainty can trigger cross-border spillover effects through trade and financial linkages.

In particular, the carry trade unwinding in early August 2024—when stock market volatility spiked in Japan and the United States (Figure ES.3) and global asset prices declined significantly—provided a glimpse of the kind of stress that can ensue when spikes in volatility interact with market reactions and hasten sell-offs.

These mounting vulnerabilities, which have become more probable to deliver adverse shocks, which have become more probable due to elevated economic, geopolitical risks, and uncertainty of the policymakers, considering that the financial markets are now fully priced as baseline outcome and that the distribution of outcomes has become wider, require that policymakers be on guard.
