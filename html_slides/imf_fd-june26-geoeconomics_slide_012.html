Rethinking
Free Trade
Kim Ruhl
US policymakers are rebalancing economic efficiency with national security amid rising geopolitical risk

When it comes to international trade, countries have always weighed economic efficiency against national security. After World War II, they pursued free international trade through low tariffs in the belief that this was both economically beneficial and politically stabilizing. World trade tripled as a share of GDP between 1950 and its peak in 2008, with about half of this trade in intermediate goods, reflecting the importance of cross-border production relationships. Although conflict continued, there were no global-scale wars like those that characterized the first half of the 20th century. The combination and stability had won out.

The world is now reevaluating the role of economic interconnectedness in global affairs, mindful that more interconnection means more dependencies that adversarial nations can exploit to get their way in other areas of international relations. Resilient economies need many characteristics: Each country must have access to the resources it needs to fight a war or survive a crisis, it must have a reliable supply of medicines, microchips, critical minerals, and other vital inputs, regardless of changing global dynamics. And it must be able to rapidly increase production in response to an emergency (think COVID-19).

The US must now grapple with the national security risks that accompany key supply chains dominated by their adversaries. The geopolitical considerations of what we trade, and who we trade with, are now a priority.

This does not repudiate comparative advantage and the gains from trade; it is an acknowledgment that free trade is not always appropriate. Free trade in well-functioning markets is still the ideal and should be pursued wherever possible, particularly with allies. Yet many of the goods the US imports are the result of deliberate nonmarket forces, which distort production and consumption despite prevailing nominal rates.

Decades of fragility

Trade and investment progressed largely unchecked. In search of efficiencies, supply chains—and entire industries—moved abroad to their lowest-cost locations. Trade policy played a role, as did technological advances in communications, transportation, and logistics that made long-distance production relationships feasible. Differences in environmental and labor standards incentivized firms to relocate production to places that value the environment and workers' rights less.

The US-led international order provided the stability that let the world's complex networks flourish. As supply chains stretched and concentrated, fragilities accumulated. The most critical were always present but often manifested in limited or idiosyncratic ways.

A series of recent shocks raised awareness of these vulnerabilities and renewed interest in how economics and national security interact.

COVID-19 supply-chain disruptions made it obvious to all that critical goods—things like pharmaceuticals, chip semiconductors, and medical supplies—came from a handful of countries and that major disruptions could become extremely painful. Supply-chain vulnerabilities surprised some companies. A Defense survey found that one-third of chief procurement officers could see the risks beyond their direct suppliers.

Europe's dependency on Russian energy reminded the world of the long-understood idea that economic integration can bind countries together in mutual restraint, but it produces leverage, too. In 2021 Russia accounted for 27 percent of oil imports and 45 percent of gas imports, according to the European Commission. By 2023, Russia accounted for 3 percent of oil and 13 percent of gas imports. Decoupling from Russian energy came at the cost of higher energy prices and slower economic growth. Higher energy bills cut incomes by about €2,000 per person in 2021-22, the Commission estimates.

Chinese export licensing controls imposed in February 2023 and a blockage of rare earths and derivatives that threatened to shutter automotive, defense, electronics, and other production lines in the US and elsewhere. Six months later, China threatened to expand the scope and scale of its export controls in a stark reminder to the US of its vulnerability.

The US must now grapple with the national security risks that accompany key supply chains dominated by their adversaries. The geopolitical considerations of what we trade, and who we trade with, have become a priority.
