Global Financial Stability Report: Financial and Climate Policies for a High-Interest-Rate Era

There is also an urgent need to address systemic risks related to commodity prices and capital flows from environmental, social, and governance-focused institutions. This can be achieved by broadening the reach of macroprudential tools and enhancing data collection.

Although the acute stress in the global banking system observed last March has subsided, a deeper look reveals that a sizable tail of weak banks remains (see Chapter 2 of this Global Financial Stability Report). Supervisors should ensure that banks have corporate governance and risk management processes commensurate with their risk profile and pay specific attention to bank asset classification and provisions, as well as exposures to interest rate and liquidity risks. Timely, intensive, and conclusive banking supervision is therefore, critical. We also urge strengthening the operational independence of supervisors by providing them clear safety and soundness mandates, adequate resources, and legal protection. Countries should also

foster growth in green finance and private capital flows to clean energy in emerging market and developing economies (EMDEs) to address climate change. Despite strong commitments from financial institutions, flows remain inadequate (see Chapter 3 of this Global Financial Stability Report). There are also risks that the energy transition in EMDEs could be very slow, given financing constraints and coal dependence. To channel more private finance toward climate investments in EMDEs, policymakers should prioritize more credible climate policies, strengthen sustainable finance frameworks and standards, and catalyze private finance through the international financial institutions and development finance institutions.

The IMF is committed to supporting our members in their efforts to address these challenges. We believe that this edition of the Global Financial Stability Report can contribute to the global policy discussion to help prevent a materialization of the financial stability risks described herein and strengthen the foundations of a sustainable and inclusive global recovery.
